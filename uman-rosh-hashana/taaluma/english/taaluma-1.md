# Part I - Recalculating the Path

## The Gap in the Heart

**Night.**  
A cool breeze slips in through the half-open shutters. The clock reads 2:17 a.m. Shlomo sits alone in the small living room, a cup of tea now almost cold by his side, and a messy pile of open books before him. He pores again and again over a passage in the book _Kokhavei Or_:

> ### Kokhavei Or – _Sasson veSimcha_, Note 3
> 
> …**Because even though the main exercise of choosing to understand on one’s own is found only among the truly great—those of the stature of Moses, who added an extra day by his own insight—nevertheless even ordinary people possess a small measure of this, for this is the very root of each person’s free will** (and for this reason our Admo"r z"l so greatly exhorted us to cry out to the Hashem for the rectification of our power of counsel). And certainly, all the more so, in something as awesome and lofty as this, one must employ that free will more and more. And indeed, it is understood as well from the words of Moharnat z”l (Reb Noson) in person—as I heard regarding this matter—that it was for _this_ reason that even the holy Rosh Hashanah after the Rebbe’s passing – traveling to Uman – **he did not explicitly reveal**.

He stops at the last sentence. _“He did not explicitly reveal”…_ Immediately, as if the doubt were dictated to him from Above, his eyes shift to another passage he had noted in his personal journal:

> ### Bi’ur HaLikutim 61, sec. 61
> 
> In passing, I will present that which I heard from my father z”l in the name of Moharnat z”l: **that no clear ruling for the above law and judgment was found, except in three things**. The obligation to study the legal codes every single day. And the obligation of prayer and supplication – to renew it each day according to whatever one is going through. **And the obligation of gathering the souls together on Rosh Hashanah at the place of his holy resting.** Thus far his words…

**Obligation?** **Clear ruling?** How can this be? On one hand – _“he did not explicitly reveal.”_ On the other – a _definitive ruling_ as clear as day? Shlomo freezes. He feels as if he has fallen into a crack between two worlds. How is it possible? Both _unsaid_ and _clear as the sun_? He closes the book, a pensive look on his face. A delicate chord within him suddenly stirs. The contradiction between the two sources echoed the contradiction in his own heart. For years he has traveled to Uman, but inside… something has dried up. He’s no longer moved. Not like before. The excitement turned into habit, and the habit—into a burden.

The gap between the two sources was the gap in his heart. On one hand, it’s an explicit directive – you simply do it. On the other hand – if the heart isn’t in it, it’s like a body without a soul… And suddenly something lit up in his heart: _If I can manage to understand this contradiction, if I can understand how Reb Noson attained such certainty – despite the Rebbe not commanding it outright – maybe… maybe my own heart will be renewed as well._

But Shlomo knew: he could not unravel this riddle on his own. He needed someone who knows how to listen to hints, who knows how to read between the lines, who can hear things that weren’t explicitly said. He needed Shmuel.

## Things They Didn’t Notice Then

A door opens. Shmuel stands in the doorway, half-asleep, half-concerned. “Shlomo?.. Do you know what time it is?”

“I’m sorry… I thought maybe you were up… It’s just that I have a question that’s really bothering me.” Shmuel looks at Shlomo with curiosity, then opens the door wide. Shlomo, stepping in, opens the two books he brought with him. “Read this,” he says, spreading out the marked pages and pointing first to the line in _Kokhavei Or_, then to the one in _Bi’ur HaLikutim_.

Shmuel reads them. Once. Twice.

“Well?.. Do you understand now?” Shlomo can barely contain his urgency. “On one hand – the Rebbe _did not say_. On the other – this is one of the three absolutely clear rulings to Reb Noson! How can that be?”

Shmuel is silent. He takes a sip from the tea sitting on the table, and gives a slight nod. “It’s a strong question… very strong.”

Shlomo gazes at him. “You know? I feel it’s not just about sources. It’s like… a calling of sorts… I feel I owe it to myself to clarify what this whole inyan of going to Uman is about… I’m a Breslover chassid like you, Shmuel, but one can’t deny that I’ve grown _old_ about Uman Rosh Hashanah as the years go by…”

Shmuel smiles. “The fact that you’re asking – that means you’re already on the right track.”

Shlomo smiles, relieved. “Listen… to understand how Reb Noson grasped what he did, we need to read about what _he_ went through…”

Shmuel stands up decisively from the table, walks over to a shelf, and pulls out a volume of _Yemei Moharnat_ – a brown hardcover, shiny but a bit worn. A few quiet minutes pass. Shmuel flips through the pages in silence, and Shlomo watches him in admiration. Then, suddenly, Shmuel stops. “Here it is.” He begins to read in a low voice, as if he were reliving Reb Noson’s own thoughts:

> ### _Yemei Moharnat_, Letter 67
> 
> Now it is fitting for me to recount all that transpired with me from the day the Rebbe passed away peacefully… **I also immediately strengthened myself with the realization that we must gather together on Rosh Hashanah in Uman, just as in the days of his life, and I had several clear proofs for this from his holy words**, for his way, of blessed memory, was exceedingly wondrous and awesome – especially regarding his instructions to us on how to conduct ourselves after his passing. For near his very passing we heard from him only a few brief words (some of which were recorded above), and he did not then command us any specific practice for how to conduct ourselves after he was gone. But in truth there is nothing in the world that he did not arrange well for us, and did not instruct us about properly, **for indeed, in all of the words that he ever spoke to us in all the days we were privileged to stand before him, through all of them he taught us how to conduct ourselves at all times, all the days of our lives – both during his lifetime and after his passing. In particular, all the discourses and conversations and lessons and stories that we heard from him from the day he came back from Lemberg – all of them were instructions teaching us how always to behave.** It was only that during his lifetime there were some words whose full depth I did not grasp, but his holy words are alive and enduring forever; they are a welling water – deep waters of advice in a man’s heart – and they are hidden in my heart. And each time, they well up as living waters and teach us and remind us how to act in every matter, and each time I come to understand retrospectively from his holy words how to conduct myself. And especially, there were many things that he explicitly said before several people – only they did not pay attention at the time. Only after his passing did we understand and see that he had **explicitly** commanded us regarding these matters, and all this cannot be adequately explained in writing…

Shmuel lifts his eyes from the text. “You see, Shlomo? Reb Noson suddenly _saw_ how all of the Rebbe’s words had in fact been instructions all along – only he hadn’t noticed at the time.”

Shlomo ponders for a moment. “So basically… the Rebbe _did_ reveal it, but it had to arise from _Reb Noson’s_ own heart?”

Shmuel nods. “Yes… True devotion to the Rebbe is not only obeying when he issues an explicit command – but even when he doesn’t. When it’s left as _a matter of choice_.”

Shlomo peers thoughtfully at the books. “So… the contradiction isn’t really a contradiction. It’s a test…”

Shmuel smiles. “Exactly. And it’s specifically after the Rebbe’s passing that his words become clarified.”

The room sinks into quiet. The only sound is the soft flutter of pages turning themselves in the gentle night breeze. Shlomo is beginning to understand… Reb Noson had learned so many teachings from the Rebbe… so many conversations, so many talks and stories… And then, the void the Rebbe left in the world opened within Reb Noson a gate to a new understanding—deeper, more authentic.

## The Call Between the Lines

Shlomo suddenly stands up and turns to his friend. “Shmuel, I feel like this is calling out to me… You know, I don’t _have_ to investigate this. I could just go, like I’ve done all these years… but I feel I didn’t end up here tonight for nothing.”

Shmuel looks at his friend, appreciating the innocence and pure yearning shining from him.

“Listen, I’m no great scholar,” Shlomo says, “but whatever I _can_ understand, I want to understand… And I think you should help me.”

“Pshh… how did you get to that conclusion?”. Shlomo smiles sheepishly.

“Don’t worry,” Shmuel replies. “Believe me, I also need to be rejuvenated in this. True, I learn the Rebbe’s and his disciples’ writings, but I’ve never focused on the subject of Rosh Hashanah. Thanks to you, with Hashem’s help, maybe I’ll merit to do so now.”

Shmuel straightens up with resolve. “We need to begin from the year _Taksu_ – 5566,” he says, setting down his empty cup.

Shlomo looks at him quizzically. “Why there, of all places?”

Shmuel opens _Chayei Moharan_ and rapidly flips through the pages. Casting a knowing glance at Shlomo, he continues, “Because something new began then…” He points to the passage:

> ### Chayei Moharan #151 – Summer תקס"ו
> 
> _His journey to Novoritch, Zaslav, Dubno and Brod:_  
> In the year תקס"ו (which was the fourth year of his dwelling here in Breslov, and the fourth year since I drew close to him—for when he first arrived here I immediately drew close to him, as mentioned above), then, in that summer of תקס"ו, in the month of Sivan, **his young son, Shlomo Efraim z"l, passed away**. And it has already been explained elsewhere regarding this precious, holy child what our Rebbe z"l, said – that he had already been prepared, etc. And then, after the passing of that child, when we came to him, **then he began to speak with us about the matter of the rectification of souls, concerning the concept of the “Master of the Field.”** He said there is a field where beautiful and pleasant trees and grasses grow, and in that field grow souls, and it needs a Master of the Field to tend and rectify them. And whoever girds himself to be the Master of the Field must endure very many and immeasurable sufferings, as explained in the Torah “And Boaz said to Ruth,” in Likutei Moharan I, Torah 65 – see there…

Shlomo reads these words softly. “Do you think this is connected to the child’s death?”

“We can’t know for sure…” Shmuel says gravely, “but we _do_ know that as long as the child lived, there was hope that Mashiach might come. But after the boy passed – the Rebbe changed course. He began to speak of rectifying the disembodied souls.”

Shmuel then opens _Yemei Moharnat_ again. He runs his finger down the lines until he finds the next reference:

> ### Yemei Moharnat, Part I, Letter 11
> 
> Also in that winter of the aforementioned year תקס"ו, I merited to write before him the book that was later burned … **but he warned me very strongly to pray for his infant son – that holy child Shlomo Efraim z"l – and also he commanded and urged the man through whose hand that manuscript was delivered, for the sake of Hashem, to pray for that infant. For he knew that they would “take upon themselves” that infant; and due to our many sins, our prayers did not avail, and the child passed away after Shavuot of that summer.** And regarding this, there are many stories… and little by little they will be explained elsewhere (see _Chayei Moharan_ #28, 151). May Hashem comfort him and us and all Israel for this great sorrow and calamity that occurred in the world, as he said explicitly; and may He console us doubly, and bring us our righteous Mashiach speedily in our days, Amen.  
> _The copyist writes:_ I saw fit to copy here what I found in the bundle of writings, and this is what it said, in his words: Shortly before the passing of that holy and awesome child of admo"r z”l, we were with the Rebbe in the attic above his house, and he told us about the greatness of his anguish and the very great and mighty sufferings that beset him from every side and angle… And in the midst of his words he exclaimed and said, **“What do _you_ know of how great and tremendous this shattering is that has happened in the world—the matter of the passing of that child? All my heart is broken and torn from its place!”** And the holy tears began to flow down his cheeks, and immediately we fled from before him, from the immense shame that fell upon us at seeing him weep in front of us; and it seemed to us as if the entire world had turned upside down and been destroyed. **Afterwards, on the next day – Friday, the eve of Shabbat Kodesh – he told us that had we not run out immediately, he would have told us something very beautiful (in Yiddish he said: “Volt _ikh_ aykh geven dertseylt _sheyns_” – _“I would have told you the most beautiful [story].”_) And on the following Friday, he delivered a wondrous Torah (that is, the Torah _“And Boaz said,”_ Torah 65).** …  
> Furthermore I found written there, and this too is in his words: In the year תקס"ו, on Sunday the 5th of Menachem Av, we heard from his holy mouth the entire order of the coming of the Righteous Mashiach (may it be speedily in our days). And he said that it had already been ready for him to come within a certain number of years, and he knew in which year and in which month and on which day he would come; **but now certainly he will not come at that time, and from his words it was understood that the delay was due to the fact that his little son, Shlomo Efraim z”l, had passed away.** Earlier as well, he had told me – shortly after that child passed away – something to this effect, saying that it had already been possible that Mashiach would come in just a few more years, and he knew in which month etc., **but now certainly he will not come then.**

Shlomo lingers on the line _“All my heart is broken and torn from its place.”_ “I don’t recall ever reading that the Rebbe cried so much…”

“Yes, nor do I… It must have been an immense tragedy…” Shmuel says quietly.

Shlomo pauses. “So how do you think this is connected to the Master of the Field? To the souls?”

“That’s what we need to uncover, Shlomo… But as we’re talking now, it occurs to me that once it was no longer possible for Mashiach to come when he was supposed to… a new path had to begin. Like a heavenly ‘recalculating of the route’ – a new way for Mashiach to come now…”

“Yes, that makes sense… That's the Inyan of the Master of the Field, and now the souls must be rectified so that Mashiach can arrive.”

Shmuel nods in agreement.

## Master of the Field

“We should learn Torah 65 now,” Shmuel says to his friend.

“Yes… that seems like the logical next step.”

Shmuel takes out his copy of _Likutei Moharan_. Its pages are creased from heavy use, filled with highlights and bookmarks.

Shlomo opens to Torah 65 and begins to read, as if entering another world.

> ### Likutei Moharan I: Torah 65 (Sections 1–2)
> 
> Know: there is a field, and in it grow very beautiful and pleasant trees and grasses. The exquisite beauty of that field and its flora is impossible to convey; fortunate is the eye that has seen it. And those trees and grasses are aspects of holy souls that grow there… **And whoever wishes to gird his loins and enter in order to become the Master of the Field must be a person who is stalwart and resolute, a mighty warrior, and a very great tzaddik. For one must be an exceedingly great and lofty person.** … And know that when the souls produce fruits – that is, when they perform the will of the Creator – then they illuminate the eyes of the Master of the Field, and thus he can be a seer and look to see to whatever is needed… **And when his eyes are illuminated and seeing, in the aspect of the above-mentioned “Field of Vision,” then he is able to look upon each and every individual, to bring him to the ultimate purpose**…

“_Master of the Field… to bring him to the ultimate purpose…_ Shmuel, what does all this mean?” Shlomo asks. “I remember learning these concepts back when I was younger, but I feel I need to look at them anew… to recalculate the path.”

Shlomo suddenly lowers his gaze. “You know… I’m also beginning to ask myself… am I even aimed toward that _ultimate purpose_? I pray, I do mitzvot, but am I really heading in the right direction?”

Shmuel smiles, moved by the innocence that wells from his friend’s heart. “Yes… I suspect we’ll end up discovering _ourselves_ on this journey – not only how Reb Noson came to understand what he did about Rosh Hashanah. And as for your question, we’ll have to turn to Reb Noson…”

Shmuel pulls from the old cupboard a volume of _Likutei Halachot_ on the Torah, _Hilchot Arvit_ #4 (in Orach Chaim, vol. II). He studies the book for a short while, and Shlomo looks on, trying to assist however he can.

## Two Ways to Reach the Ultimate Purpose

“The picture is starting to become clearer…” Shmuel says suddenly.

“Yes… I’m beginning to recall..”. Shlomo watches his old friend with anticipation.

“See, Shlomo, there are very elevated concepts of Kabbalah and Chassidut here… but in simple terms, Reb Noson explains that there are two ways to become included in the _tachlit_ – the ultimate purpose, the Infinite.”

> ### Likutei Halachot, Hilchot Arvit IV, Section 7
> 
> …**For in truth, every person is required to sacrifice his life for the sake of Hashem, and to shut his eyes completely from beholding this world**, meaning: to utterly break all his worldly desires, completely and absolutely, and to accept upon himself all forms of affliction and self-mortification for the sake of Hashem – just as Rabbi Akiva, whose flesh was combed with iron combs until his soul departed on the word “One” (echad). And only then, _precisely then_, will he merit to apprehend Hashem’s Divinity to the ultimate degree. Fortunate is he who attains this. **But not everyone is able to withstand this – to break everything and nullify himself utterly. Therefore Hashem had mercy on us and gave us His Torah, which is a Tree of Life to those who hold fast to it… and its ways are ways of pleasantness.** That is, through the Torah one can merit to know Hashem, by fulfilling all the commandments and engaging in Torah study all his days. Then one can merit eternal life, even though he did not completely break all his desires.

“The way of the tzaddikim like Rabbi Akiva, and the way of the rest of the people – our way,” Shlomo nods in understanding.

“Hold on,” Shmuel says, knitting his brow. “Wait, I don’t understand…”

“What is it?” Shlomo asks, snapping back to attention.

“Look what it says here:” Shmuel points at the text.

> ### Likutei Halachot, Hilchot Arvit IV, Section 9
> 
> **But when a person, God forbid, transgresses some directive of the Torah, then he damages the conduit and the vessel through which the light of the revelation of Hashem’s Divinity is received (from the aforementioned Reshimu). If so, how is it possible to repair this? He must be repaired by means of _teshuvah_ (repentance), and teshuvah is higher than the Torah – meaning that because he transgressed the Torah, he must now perform teshuvah.** And teshuvah consists of shutting one’s eyes completely to the sights of this world. This corresponds to the fasting and self-mortifications imposed upon penitents. **For since he transgressed the words of Torah, he needs to ascend to a state of absolute self-nullification – to accept upon himself fasts and afflictions and to completely close his eyes to this world – until he merits to reach the aforementioned level of nullification, and to gaze upon the ultimate purpose which is entirely good and entirely One.** And there, in that aforementioned state of nullification, there it is above the Torah. And therefore, it is precisely there that one can repair what was blemished and ruined by transgressing the Torah. For there it is higher than Torah – since all of Torah is drawn from there. **And so he can draw from there lights and rectifications to repair what he had damaged in the words of Torah, as mentioned above.** …

“Do you see?” Shmuel says, excited. “Earlier Reb Noson wrote that not everyone merits that first path – but if someone _does_ transgress the Torah, then he _does_ have to reach that level in order to do teshuvah. How does that square with what we read before?..”

“Great,” Shlomo chuckles wearily. “Another _kushia_ (difficulty)…”

Shmuel grins and immediately continues pondering in his mind.

## Where to Begin

“But wait,” Shmuel says, suddenly turning back to the book, “what does he write a bit further on?”

Shmuel’s eyes skim swiftly over the pages. He even begins to gently rock the volume back and forth in his hands, eyebrows furrowed. Shlomo watches his friend with curiosity, careful not to interrupt.

All at once, Shmuel sighs in relief. “I’ve got it… This is about Rosh Hashanah! Thanks to you, Shlomo!”

Shlomo blushes. “What do you mean?”

“You know, sustenance is being sent down to me from Heaven on account of your pure desire – Heavenly insights that, in my own capacity, I might not have received…”

“No, no…” Shlomo laughs. “I meant – what do you mean that _this_ is the matter of Rosh Hashanah?”

“Ohh,” Shmuel laughs as well. “_That’s_ what you meant… Take a look at what Reb Noson writes in section 13.”

> ### Likutei Halachot, Hilchot Arvit IV, Section 13
> 
> For this is a general principle explained above: that all illuminations, perceptions, and revelations of Godliness are drawn into the world only by means of the light that the true tzaddikim apprehend through the state of _bitul_ (self-nullification), as mentioned earlier. But this great and awesome light cannot be received except through many vessels, as stated. However, in truth, the very work of preparing and making those vessels is itself drawn from that aforementioned light of _bitul_. For in reality it is all one – the lights and the vessels all derive from one place, from the Infinite Light, where there is the aspect of absolute _bitul_. **And therefore, in truth, this matter is “wondrous, perfect in knowledge” – a wondrous paradox – for one cannot draw the light except by means of vessels, and one cannot fashion vessels except by drawing down the light from that place. So then, from where is the beginning of the effort to effect this rectification? And therefore, in truth, the matter of Rosh Hashanah is a very wondrous and utterly hidden mystery – for as we understood from the words of Admo"r z"l, about the exceedingly great and awesome importance of Rosh Hashanah, he said that _all his Inyan is Rosh Hashanah_.** …

“Here Reb Noson begins explaining Rosh Hashanah to us… and why the Rebbe said that all his Inyan is Rosh Hashanah!” Shmuel remarks.

Shlomo listens closely. “He’s saying that Rosh Hashanah is a wondrous matter – because we can’t tell where the beginning lies… To make vessels you need lights, and to get lights, you need… vessels. **And so** Rosh Hashanah is utterly wondrous and concealed, and **therefore** the Rebbe told them that Rosh Hashanah is great, tremendous and awesome, and that all his Inyan is Rosh Hashana!”

Shlomo reflects a moment. “I understand… this is essentially the _secret_ of Rosh Hashanah! Reb Noson is revealing to us the secret – what the Rebbe achieved, and why Rosh Hashanah is all of his Inyan…”

“Right… Reb Noson is revealing the secret. But at the same time, he’s letting us know that it remains a wonder – beyond _any_ human understanding. No human intellect, however brilliant, can resolve this paradox. If lights are needed for vessels and vessels are needed for lights, then not only can’t we tell where the beginning is – we are forced to admit that **there _is_ no** beginning, not by the grasp of human reason.”

Shlomo stares at his friend in amazement. “Then how is there _ever_ a beginning? We know the Rebbe **does** accomplish Rosh Hashanah, regardless – right?”

“Exactly… So _how_?” Shmuel raises a finger. “Give me one second.” He scans a bit further. “Ah, here it is. Reb Noson explains it in the next section.”

> ### Likutei Halachot, Hilchot Arvit IV, Section 14
> 
> …And on Rosh Hashanah takes place the rectification of the sin of Adam HaRishon, and of all sins in every generation. For Rosh Hashanah is the very day Adam HaRishon was created and on which he sinned by the Tree of Knowledge. And it is the first of the Ten Days of Teshuvah, the first day of the year. **And at that time, the essential rectification is accomplished by the true tzaddik of the generation, who by the great magnitude of his righteousness and holiness is able to utterly nullify himself in absolute _bitul_. For he completely shuts his eyes from beholding this world, and has no gaze upon this world at all. He even accepts upon himself all torments and sufferings harder than death for the sake of Hashem and for the sake of Israel, until he ascends and is able to gaze upon the ultimate purpose in a state of absolute self-nullification,** attaining a most lofty and wondrous level—until he merits to draw down from there restorations to reveal Hashem’s Divinity, so that he awakens even those who are very, very distant, bringing them to repentance.**  
> **And the main wonders of his work are on Rosh Hashanah, for the main point is the beginning – which is the aspect of Rosh Hashanah, the beginning of Creation and the beginning of repentance (being day one of the Ten Days of Teshuvah). For on that day his work is amazingly wondrous and awesome due to the above concept – for he must draw down all the rectifications anew, and it is hidden and wondrous as to where the beginning lies, since one cannot draw down lights except via vessels, and cannot form vessels except via drawing down lights, as above.** And if Adam had not sinned, he would have rectified all of this in a single day. But now, after the sin – when he reverted and corrupted everything, and not only failed to rectify but even spoiled further – therefore it is impossible now to rectify everything in one day, as mentioned. Thus the rectification must be done gradually, over multiple days. And this is the aspect of the Ten Days of Teshuvah from Rosh Hashanah through Yom Kippur. For Rosh Hashanah is the beginning which is extremely difficult, weighty, and exceedingly wondrous, **for one must ascend to the ultimate _bitul_ and then immediately return from that place, to draw the light back down gradually and with constriction, solely in order to begin to repair even the tiniest vessel**, so that we can begin to draw down the revelation of Hashem's light. …

“Reb Noson here is explaining what Rosh Hashanah is all about,” Shmuel says. “We don’t know where the beginning comes from, and essentially, by human logic… it’s impossible to even start. But one who _completely closed his eyes_ to this world – one who utterly nullified himself, and accepted upon himself sufferings **harder than death** for the sake of Hashem and for the Jewish people – such a one ascends to a place from which everything begins… and there, he can grant everyone, _including us_, a new beginning.”

“Wow….” Shlomo breathes, letting the new information sink in.

“Hold on,” Shlomo suddenly rouses himself. “What about our original question? How can an ordinary person do _teshuvah_ at all, if doing teshuvah depends on ascending to the ultimate _bitul_, and not everyone is capable of that?”

Shmuel smiles. “Ah, I see you’re keeping up. Pay attention to what Reb Noson explains back in section 9.”

> ### _(Revisiting Hilchot Arvit IV,)_ Section 9 (excerpt)
> 
> …And there, in that aforementioned state of _bitul_, there it is above the Torah. And therefore, it is precisely there that one can rectify what was blemished and ruined by transgressing the Torah, for there it is above the Torah – since all of Torah is drawn from there. **And thus one can draw from there lights and restorations to repair what was damaged in the words of Torah, as explained above.** …

“The essence of teshuvah is drawing down rectifications,” Shmuel explains gently. “And this tzaddik who nullified himself in total _bitul_—he merits to draw those rectifications down _from that place_ for us!”

“Pshhh… what a revelation,” Shlomo says, shaking his head in wonder.

“Wait, now suddenly _I_ understand something!” he blurts.

Shlomo jumps up and hurries to the old bookcase, scanning the shelves.

“What are you looking for? I’ll help you,” Shmuel offers, intrigued.

“No need, one second… Here, I found it,” Shlomo says, pulling out a copy of _Chayei Moharan_. “Now **I** am going to show _you_ something powerful.” He smiles.

Shmuel moves closer, interest shining in his eyes, and looks at the page Shlomo has opened.

> ### Chayei Moharan #291 (5566)
> 
> In the year 5566, during the days between Keseh and Asor (between Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur), Rabenu z"l came up from the mikveh and said that at that moment it had been revealed to him from Heaven how our circle (“Anshei Shlomeinu”) should conduct themselves regarding fasts for the rest of their lives – how many fasts each and every one of our men ought to fast each year, and how and when they should fast… And we understood from his words that these fasts were precious matters and very lofty practices; fortunate are we to have merited to receive them. And this practice continued for about two years.  
> **After that, when he returned from Lemberg, he commanded us not to fast any longer at all. And he said: Indeed, whoever does not merit to be with me on Rosh Hashanah has no need to fast whatsoever, since it won't benefit him, as above. And whoever _does_ merit to be with me on Rosh Hashanah certainly need not fast at all, since he merits to be with me on Rosh Hashanah – understand this well.**

Shmuel gazes at the open book Shlomo has handed him, awe and reverence intermingling on his face. “Now I understand… The Rebbe revealed the secret of Rosh Hashanah when he returned from the Holy Land. But his Rosh Hashanah _grew_. In the beginning, he knew exactly what they needed to rectify, in detail – but later… they just needed to _come_.”

Shlomo looks at his friend and smiles. “Well, I did manage to teach you something new, didn’t I…?”

Shmuel smiles back. Suddenly, the cry of a baby pierces the air from the next room. “I can’t believe it… It’s already 7:00 in the morning! I have to get the kids to school, and I haven’t even davened Shacharit yet!”

Shlomo glances toward the window in surprise and then back at Shmuel, a bit disappointed. “Right now? We’re in the middle of the climax… We still haven’t figured out how all this connects to the **Tzaddik’s grave**… After all, Reb Noson wrote that lesson in Hilchot Arvit #4 a full 12 years after the Rebbe’s passing…!”

Shmuel puts a reassuring hand on his friend’s shoulder and smiles. “_‘For you shall not go out in haste…’_ Don’t worry. We’ll get to that part too.”

Shlomo sighs softly. “You’re right… ‘Not in haste.’ No rushing the moment. Hashem is leading us, and Rabenu is with us. Good luck with your little ones – I also need to grab a quick prayer. I just hope I’ll stay awake…”

Shmuel gives his friend a warm pat on the back and escorts him to the door. The two good friends part ways in peace, each buoyed by hope – their path recalculating, and their hearts already beginning to rekindle with new light.

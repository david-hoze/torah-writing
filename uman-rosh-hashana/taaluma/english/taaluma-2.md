# Part Two – Greater Are Tzaddikim in their demise

## A paid babysitter

Shlomo spent the next few days with questions running through his head. He tried learning some sources on his own, but very quickly realized he still needed Shmuel’s help. He pulled his phone out of his pocket and dialed his good friend. “Yes, Shlomo... I’ve been waiting for your call,” came Shmuel’s voice. Shlomo sighed in relief and smiled. “Oh, I’m glad to hear that. Listen, we absolutely have to continue... I can’t make heads or tails of this on my own.” Shmuel chuckled on the other end. “I understand... I just need to find some time...”. Shmuel thought for a few moments, and then answered, “Let’s meet after Ma’ariv tomorrow. I can clear a few hours – after all, these matters are of cosmic importance.” Shlomo grinned. “Alright. I’ve waited this long; I can hold out until tomorrow night.”

The next day at Ma’ariv, Shlomo made sure to spot Shmuel in the minyan at the synagogue. He tried to focus on the Shemoneh Esrei prayer and not peek to check if Shmuel was still within sight. He waited patiently until the final Kaddish, and immediately went over to his dear friend. “Oh, Shlomo!” Shmuel said happily at the sight of him. “How are you? Yes, yes, I’ve been waiting for you too. Let’s head to my place – they’re about to turn off the lights here in shul.”

Shlomo entered Shmuel’s home and followed him into the small study off to the side of the house. He was surprised to see Yossi there – a 17-year-old from their community who wasn’t exactly known for fitting in. Yossi was dressed as usual in a white short-sleeved shirt with a white Chassidic yarmulke, his long **peyot** (sidelocks) dangling behind his ears. “Oh, great, they stuck me with the two most boring guys in the community,” Yossi muttered aloud to himself. Shlomo swallowed hard in surprise. But Shmuel immediately put a hand on Yossi’s shoulder and said, “Hey, Yossi, how’s it going? Shlomo,” he turned to his friend, “you don’t mind if Yossi hangs out with us for a bit, right? I promised his father I’d spend some time with him.” Shlomo paused a moment to process the situation.

“Don’t worry, Shloimele, I won’t bother you much...” Yossi said with a sarcastic grin. “See, I’m stepping out for a smoke now, so you two can go on with your riveting discussions...” Shlomo raised his eyebrows and gave a hesitant smile. Yossi headed toward the balcony and pulled a pack of cigarettes from his pocket. “Don’t forget to shut the door tight so the smell doesn’t get in,” Shmuel called after Yossi, who was already outside. Yossi closed the porch door firmly and turned his face outward toward the black night sky.

## No Tzaddik Ever Knew

“Don’t pay attention, Shlomo... Yossi’s a good guy, but he’s been through a few yeshivas and had a rough time. He didn’t exactly fit in, to put it mildly, and it wasn’t always his fault...”

“Yeah, I understand,” Shlomo said warmly. “I’m Dan to Kaf Zchut, don’t worry.”

“Alright, so what have we come up with so far?” Shmuel asked his friend.

“Look,” Shlomo began, still recovering from the surprising encounter with the 17-year-old, “I’m a bit confused... R’ Natan writes here: _‘And then the main rectification is accomplished by the true tzaddik of the generation, who, through his great righteousness and holiness, is able to nullify himself completely. He shuts his eyes completely against any sight of this world, and has no concern for this world at all. He even takes upon himself all the afflictions and all the sufferings, harder than death, for the sake of Hashem and for the sake of Israel—until he merits to ascend and gaze upon the ultimate purpose in a state of absolute self-nullification, at a very, very high and wondrous level. At that point he merits to draw down rectifications from there, to reveal Hashem’s divinity, until he can awaken even all those who are extremely distant to return in repentance.’_”

“Right,” Shmuel responded.

“I don’t get it... Is he referring to **Rabbeinu** (Rebbe Nachman)?” Shlomo asked.

“Pshh... you’re asking excellent questions. _Yasher koach_ — that really helps me focus,” Shmuel said appreciatively. “There’s a very fundamental piece by R’ Avraham b’Reb Nachman on this topic. Here, come take a look.” Shmuel walked over to the small wooden bookcase in the room.

>Kokhavei Or – Chochmah U’Binah 20–21

>**“And regarding the fact that he resolved and took possession of the holy field to water and grow the trees (which are the souls of Israel, referred to by the names ‘field’ and ‘vineyard’)** ... also, that he strives, by means of the wondrous four powers that he possesses, to rescue and to heal all the souls of Israel, and especially, most especially, all those whom he will awaken through an awakening from below — through speech and through action (namely, by giving charity and by reciting the Ten Psalms at his holy gravesite) ... And likewise, all the other words of that praise will all be explained as referring to this matter to anyone insightful, in conjunction with the other introductions, as brought in _Likutei Halachot_, Hilchot Hashkamat HaBoker, Halacha 4 — see there. **And the discerning reader will understand on his own that whenever he (Rabbeinu) is not explicitly mentioned there — and similarly in other places in his holy writings, where only a general reference is made to a “tzaddik” — it is because of Darchei Shalom and "Kvod Hashem Haster Davar"**”

“Pshh... One has to read that introduction before studying _Likutei Halachot_,” Shlomo exclaimed.

“Indeed,” Shmuel smiled. “R’ Avraham b’Reb Nachman writes explicitly that essentially the _Master of the Field_ is Rabbeinu himself. And it even says so in _Chayei Moharan_.”

>Chayei Moharan #152

“In the aforementioned year of 5566, when he began to speak with me about the matter of the rectification of souls that he was engaged in — which is the subject of the Master of the Field, as noted — at that time he said to me: ‘Back in Zlatipolia, I came to know a bit about this. That was when I first began to understand something about this matter of the Master of the Field.’ And the implication of his words was that it was then that he started to know, but the main knowing he has is now. And he said that Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai, z”l, had hinted a little about this in the holy Zohar. **And it's now understood that the way he knows now this matter of the Master of the Field — no Tzaddik ever knew.**”

“Wow... so this is a copy of _Chayei Moharan_ with the omitted passages restored...” Shlomo marveled.

“Yes,” Shmuel affirmed. “And since a few days had passed, I remembered a pamphlet I have from Moreinu HaRav, _shlit”a_,” he said, pulling out a slim white booklet. “It’s got some very high-level Kabbalah, but I managed to find something that even we can grasp.”

_(Shmuel opened the booklet and began to read.)_

>Bi Yikhtiru Tzadikim – Sod Nekudat HaMerkaz (Teachings of our teacher HaRav _shlit”a_, delivered on Rosh Hashana 5771)
>Section 2

>**“Now we have come on Rosh Hashana to rectify the sin of Adam HaRishon and the sin of the Golden Calf,** from which all sins and blemishes stem... **And the moment Rabbeinu stepped the first four Amot onto the soil of Eretz Yisrael, the secret of Rosh Hashana was immediately revealed to him.** This secret is the rectification for the sin of the Golden Calf and the sin of the Tree of Knowledge. At that time, the 370 Lights and the 150 Lights that had been taken from each person on the first day of Creation are returned to him — for that day (the day of mankind’s creation) was designated as a day of _teshuvah_ (repentance) for all generations. However, in order to form the vessels for us so that we can receive those 370 Lights and 150 Lights, **Rabbeinu himself must create for us the vessels so that we can receive these great lights**.”

“Wow... this lines up exactly with _Likutei Halachot_, Hilchot Arvit, section 4! In that Halakha too it speaks about Adam’s sin, about Rosh Hashana, and about the vessels that the Master of the Field makes,” Shlomo said excitedly.

“Yes,” Shmuel agreed, nodding earnestly. “And the Rav goes on to write:”

>(Same pamphlet, Section 4)

>“... And it is brought in _Tikkun 69_ that Adam HaRishon cast darkness between the Cause of all Causes and the Cause of causes... and when Rabbeinu is in the aspect of Adam HaRishon [‘Adam’ being an acronym for Adam–David–Moshe], **who came after five thousand five hundred and fifty-nine years to completely rectify the sin of Adam HaRishon and to fashion for all mortals the vessels that had been nullified for them**, ...”

“Wow, what words... Rabbeinu _fully_ repaired the sin of Adam HaRishon!” Shlomo exclaimed with excitement. “I’m starting to catch the picture now...” he murmured thoughtfully.

## Another Stone in the Great Building

Yossi came back into the little room and sprawled on the small two-seater couch, trying to arrange his tall body on the narrow sofa. He put headphones on his ears and started fiddling with the music player he pulled from his pocket. Shlomo looked at the teenager with compassion, then continued speaking. “Okay. This really sheds light on the issue... R’ Natan understood the secret of Rosh Hashana, and understood more deeply that Rabbeinu is the one who _“makes”_ Rosh Hashana... But Shmuel... what happens at the **histalkut** (the tzaddik’s passing)?”

Shmuel pondered the matter seriously, and went back to examining the books. Yossi cast a glance now and then toward the two friends, but immediately returned to tinkering with his music player.

“Look,” Shmuel said suddenly, fixing a sharp gaze on Shlomo. He directed his attention to the book he was holding. “Rabbeinu already, in _Torah 65_, spoke about the passing of the Master of the Field.”

**Likutei Moharan Torah 65, Letter 1**

_“... And someone who wants to gird his loins and enter into being the Master of the Field needs to be a man of stature and strength, a mighty warrior, wise, and a very great tzaddik. For one must be an extremely and wondrously exalted person. **And there is one who cannot finish the task except through his death.** And even for that, one must be immensely great, for there are many individuals who, even with their death, would not be effective. Only if there is a person who is very, very great and extraordinary can he finish what he needs to in his lifetime. For many sufferings and terribly difficult things befall him, but through his greatness and his stature he overcomes all of them, and he carries out the works of the field as is necessary...”_

“And it’s understood from his words that in essence, after the passing, the Master of the Field can do even more — he can **finish** the job,” Shmuel explained.

“Oh, right... how did I not catch that...” Shlomo said excitedly.

Yossi snuck a peek at the two, and then went back to his music.

“How funny — it’s even written in _Chayei Moharan_! The whole idea of the Master of the Field and the rectification of souls is directly tied to Rabbeinu’s passing and his being laid to rest in the city of Uman!” Shlomo exclaimed. He quickly started searching through the pile of books on the table for the copy of _Chayei Moharan_.

**Chayei Moharan #151**

**“... And then, after the passing of that child mentioned earlier, when we came to him, he then began to speak with us about the matter of the rectification of souls in relation to the Master of the Field.** He said that there is a field in which souls grow, and they need a Master of the Field to rectify them. And one who girds his loins to be the Master of that Field is subjected to countless sufferings, without measure, as is explained in the lesson **‘And Boaz said to Ruth’** in _Likutei Moharan_ (Torah 65) – see there. **And from that time on, he spoke much about the rectification of souls, and especially after he returned from Lemberg — particularly upon his entry into Uman — for his entire coming there, and the fact that he chose to pass away there and be buried there, was all on account of this: for the sake of rectifying those souls that required rectification for many hundreds of years.** For in Uman, countless souls were killed without number, including many, many thousands of children who were killed before their time, etc., as understood from his conversations in Uman (some of which were recorded elsewhere). ...”

“And in _Yemei Moharnat_ (The Days of Moharnat), he elaborates on this even further,” Shmuel added.

**Yemei Moharnat #58**

“On Motza’ei Shabbat, the night before Monday of Sukkot, he instructed them to move him from the chair and lay him on his bed. He was extremely exhausted and worn out, and said that he was so very tired because he had sat in the chair for so long. After that, he responded and said in a somewhat weak voice, ‘Do you remember the story I told you?’ I immediately stood there trembling, and I asked him in fright, ‘Which story?’ (For in truth I had heard several extremely wondrous stories from him.) He answered and said, ‘The story I told you when I first arrived here.’ And I was immediately very alarmed, for I remembered well the story that speaks **of the Baal Shem Tov, z”l, who came to a certain place where there were very many great souls that he was compelled to rectify, and he saw that it was impossible to rectify them except by his _histalkut_ (departing this world),** as that story is recorded elsewhere (above, section 49). (He said in Yiddish: _“Mer hot shoyn lang aroisgekukt oyf mir, mer zol mich dertappen ahér; vos zol ich aych zogn – alafim, revavos, revavos, revavos...”_ and he drew out the word “revavos” (“tens of thousands”) each time, meaning:) that here in Uman there are tens upon tens of thousands of souls presently around him for him to rectify. It is for this reason that he is enduring all these unbelievably harsh torments. After saying this, he turned his face slightly toward the wall and stretched out his hands a bit, as if declaring, ‘Behold, I sacrifice my life, and I am ready and willing to accept it all for His sake Yitbarach.’ And all of this cannot possibly be described in writing at all. **For regarding the rectification of souls, we heard many awesome conversations and teachings from his own mouth, in which he hinted to us that he was deeply engaged in this — as in the lesson “And Boaz said to Ruth” (Likutei Moharan Torah 65) about the Master of the Field. And we understood from his words that all the suffering and opposition he endured was because he was so very involved in this. And the entire matter of his journey to Uman, to pass away there, was only for this purpose,** as a bit of this is recorded in his holy conversations elsewhere (Chayei Moharan #217).”

“Pshh... exactly,” said Shmuel. “Another signpost for us. We have some _si’ata d’Shmaya_ (heavenly assistance), my dear friend. It’s no coincidence that my heart led me to look at the year 5566 and the story of the passing of that holy child. This really sheds light on the whole Rosh Hashana matter.”

Shlomo was only half-listening, because some lines further down the page had caught his attention. “Shmuel... take a look at this. What does this mean?”

**(There, section 59)**

“Afterwards, on the night connected to Tuesday — which was the fourth night of Sukkot, the final night of his holy life — up until after midnight, my companions R’ Naftali and R’ Shimon were standing by him (for we were rotating shifts), and at the time we heard the above conversation, R’ Naftali was not with him because he had gone to sleep then. Afterwards, that night, I slept and he, z”l, stood by him together with R’ Shimon. And then that night he again spoke about the above-mentioned souls, how many judgments there are here, how many holy ones were here, and so on. **R’ Naftali answered and said to him: “But didn’t you say in the torah ‘And Boaz said to Ruth’ that the truly great and exalted tzaddik can finish the matter during his lifetime? (See there.)” He (Rabbeinu), z”l, answered and said: “I only told you that as one _bechina_ (one possible aspect) of it, because it is absolutely necessary to go and be _mistalek_ (depart from this world) for this.”**”

“_Yasher koach_ gadol (well done), Shlomo!” Shmuel said gratefully. Shlomo blushed.

“A _groise shkoyach_,” Yossi muttered to himself in an exaggerated tone, rolling his eyes.

Shmuel laughed lightly and shook his head, but continued speaking passionately. “Rabbeinu is saying here explicitly that what he taught about the Master of the Field being able to finish the task in his lifetime — that was only one aspect! In reality, there is an aspect in which he ultimately _must_ pass away in order to accomplish it! Rabbeinu essentially delivered that whole lesson knowing that his role, in the end, required him to pass away for it...”

“Pshhhh....” Shlomo nodded his head in amazement. Suddenly, the beginnings of an idea sparked in his mind, and he turned to Shmuel excitedly. “So at the very end of his life, he revealed to R’ Naftali that **everything** he’d been telling them all along about the Master of the Field — there’s a certain aspect of it that he _has_ to pass away for, in order to continue the rectifications! And basically, if the Master of the Field is the one who creates the vessels on Rosh Hashana, and everyone needs to come to him — ‘no one should be missing’ — then if from the outset there are aspects that he **must** complete after his passing, isn’t it a _kal va’chomer_ (all the more so) that we need to come to him even **after** his passing?!”

Shmuel thought it over. “It certainly seems logical. If Rabbeinu achieved the role of Master of the Field in a way no other tzaddik ever had — and it’s not that he needed to pass away because he wasn’t great enough to finish the work in life, but rather that there are higher, more awesome aspects for which he had to pass away — and as we know, _‘tzaddikim in their death are greater than in their life,’_ then seemingly this only strengthens the matter! On the contrary — all the more so must we come to him after his death. If Rabbeinu continues, and even **amplifies**, his work of rectification after his passing, it’s clear that we must keep coming to him. In fact, _“great are the righteous in their death”_ — their power then is even greater... It follows that the need to assemble only **increases**.”

Shlomo was breathing heavily with excitement. “And in any case, we can definitely say that this is yet another big hint that R’ Natan received — on Rabbeinu’s final day — pointing to the importance of gathering at his holy resting place on Rosh Hashana. Another stone in the great edifice that R’ Natan constructed around coming together at the tzaddik’s gravesite for Rosh Hashana.” Shlomo nodded toward his friend with a heartfelt sense of awe and conviction.

## Drawing a Great Light from the _Reshimu_

Shmuel looked at his friend and thought for a moment. “Let me take another look at that section in _Arvit 4_. I want to understand this a bit more deeply.” He returned to the small wooden table, opened some books, and delved back into his study. In the meantime, Shlomo jotted a few notes in his personal notebook, and Yossi continued to languish on the couch.

“Oh, here, take a look...” Shmuel said suddenly. “R’ Natan explains what **our** role is after the _histalkut_ (passing).”

**Likutei Halachot, Hilchot Arvit 4, Paragraph 20**

“And this is the meaning of _“And it was at the seventh day, the waters of the Flood were upon the earth…”_ – our Sages, z”l, taught that Hashem delayed the Flood for the seven days of mourning for Metushelach the Tzaddik. For it is explained in the aforementioned lesson (at the beginning) – regarding the Master of the Field who toils in rectifying the field (which is the rectification of souls) – that sometimes this Master of the Field needs to be very great and to endure many troubles and sufferings, and sometimes he even needs to **pass away** for this, as mentioned above. ... **And then everything depends on the generation that remains after him**, who need to take to heart very much the passing of the tzaddik and to be awakened to complete _teshuvah_ (repentance) because of it. **Then they merit to draw light from the _reshimu_ (the “impression” or spiritual imprint) of the tzaddik that is left behind from him.** For every tzaddik, when he passes on, certainly leaves behind some good _reshimu_ in the world through his Torah and his good deeds. **And then the main sweetening of harsh judgments is achieved by the “orphaned” generation that remains after him – for when they stir themselves to repent, then they draw a great light from his _reshimu_**, whereby all the judgments in the world are sweetened and nullified. **Because once the tzaddik has ascended on high and is now in a state of absolute self-nullification, therefore from that realm a great and wondrous light is continually drawn down to his _reshimu_ that remains in this world. However, there must, at the very least, be some awakening from below through the good deeds of the generation, in order to draw through that the light of his holy _reshimu_.** For by their good deeds – even though those deeds are in Bechinat Katnut and they have no power on their own to sweeten judgments through spiritual attainment of the Torah – nevertheless, by the power of the tzaddik who has departed, they can, through their good deeds, draw a great and wondrous light from his _reshimu_, which is now shining with an immense and awesome light, since the tzaddik is in a state of perfect self-nullification. ...”

“Wow..” Shlomo murmured in awe. “R’ Natan is explaining that now, _right at this very moment_, a great and wondrous light is shining from Rabbeinu’s _reshimu_!”

“Indeed,” Shmuel agreed.

“‘Right at this moment’...” Yossi muttered under his breath.

Shlomo smiled but ignored him. “Beautiful... by our good deeds, we draw light from Rabbeinu’s _reshimu_. But how is that connected to the _Tziun_ (the holy gravesite)?”

Shmuel paused for a moment. “That’s a strong question...” He furrowed his brow and continued thinking. “Let’s see.” He stepped over to the bookshelf and pulled out a volume of _Likutei Halachot_.

**Likutei Halachot, Hilchot Nizikin 3, Paragraph 8**

**“And this is the aspect of prostrating oneself on the graves of true tzaddikim,** for it is a very great and very effective practice for serving Hashem – to merit escaping one’s evil and returning to Hashem Yitbarach. **For there are people who have sunk so deep in their sins, and evil has attached itself to them so strongly, that it is impossible for them in any way to return to Hashem except by coming to the graves of the true tzaddikim.** Then, they become incorporated into the soul of the tzaddik who has died, who is constantly working to sift out and elevate souls. And by the tzaddik’s great power – which is greater in his death than in his life – he can purify even the souls that have fallen extremely, extremely low. **However, the Tsaddik cannot refine the Ba'al Bechira against his will; it only happens when a person comes of his own accord to the tzaddik’s grave at the site of his holy resting place and awakens himself to repentance.** Then the tzaddik has power, with his great might, to refine that person and lift him out of the place to which he had fallen, and to repair him – even though by natural means it was impossible for him ever to be rectified, as mentioned above.”

Shlomo breathed in astonishment.

Shmuel continued, “There are people for whom nothing will help them... except to come to the grave of the tzaddik. In other words, Shlomo, **of all the effects that a departed tzaddik has on us, the most powerful, the most intense influence is at the gravesite.** So much so that there are individuals for whom this, and **only** this, will accomplish their rectification...”

Shlomo nodded in silent agreement, yet it seemed something was still nagging at him.

## It’s Forbidden to Lose to Naftali Suissa

“Shmuel...” Yossi said in a plaintive tone. “You told me if I come, there’d be snacks, we’d play ping pong. So far I’ve eaten maybe half a cashew I found under the couch, and I’m being forced to listen to you guys on your magical journey to find the truth...”

Shmuel chuckled. “It **is** a wondrous journey...”

“If you weren’t so good at ping pong, trust me I wouldn’t have come. You’re lucky you have that one redeeming quality, buddy. But how long do I have to sit here getting bored... I’ve already gone through all the songs on my MP3 player.”

“You’re right, you’re right – I really did promise you...” Shmuel said, scratching his forehead. Shlomo watched the two of them with interest.

“Ping pong? I didn’t know you were into that... mind if I join?” Shlomo asked.

Shmuel smiled. “I think Yossi would be okay with that...”

“Oh, thank goodness,” Yossi said with a sigh of relief. “Finally... You’ll show me how to smash properly, right, Shmuel? How much longer can I keep losing to Naftali Suissa?”

“Yeah, you’re right,” Shmuel nodded with mock seriousness. “This really can’t go on...” he said, and led his two friends out to the porch.

Shmuel unfolded the ping pong table that was tucked behind the s’chach and wooden boards from the sukkah, and began setting up the net. “You see, Shlomo, back when I was a _bochur_ (yeshiva student) I had a lot of free time at my grandparents’ house in Zikhron Yaakov, and... a guy’s gotta unwind once in a while.”

Shlomo smiled. “It’s all good... I won’t tell the other _avreichim_ (married yeshiva fellows)...”

“Alright, I’m up first against you!” Yossi said, twirling the paddle in his hand. Shlomo watched as Shmuel and Yossi began their game, but there were still questions pecking at his mind. _“The gravesite is the most powerful... but how does that connect to the vessels..?”_ he murmured to himself.

Yossi cocked an ear in between volleys, but kept focusing on deflecting Shmuel’s shots. Shlomo continued to mumble to himself, gently rocking back and forth. _“The vessels... the gravesite... how is it connected?”_ He squeezed his eyes shut, concentrating, until suddenly he burst out in prayer, “Hashem, help me, Tatte! I don’t understand – help me understand... how is it connected?” Shlomo pleaded from the depths of his heart, tears beginning to stream from his eyes.

Yossi glanced over occasionally at the fervently praying young man between one hit and the next, feeling uncomfortable, but continued playing until he finally halted the game. “Enough, enough! You’re messing up our game! Come here,” Yossi said, grabbing one of the _Likutei Halachot_ volumes that were lying on the table. “Here, you see this? _This_ is the issue of the vessels.”

Shlomo looked at Yossi in surprise, still in shock from the abrupt turn of events.

“Pay attention, Reb Shloimele,” Yossi said, trying to snap Shlomo back to focus. “Right here – look where I’m pointing.”

“Huh?” Shlomo uttered, still dazed.

“Here, read what it says,” Yossi insisted.

**Likutei Halachot, Hilchot Mincha 7, Paragraph 47**

**“For all the true chiddushei Torah (genuine Torah insights) are an aspect of vessels and holy contractions, in the aspect of _tikkun_ (repair), so that we can receive the Infinite Light through them gradually, etc., in an aspect of repair, etc. But the Evil One (the _baal davar_) provokes [opposes] each time;** _yet nonetheless, they (the tzaddikim) complete their work. And whatever they could not complete in their lifetime, they complete after their death, for “greater are the tzaddikim in their death than in their life,” as mentioned above._ **For then they ascend to the place they ascend, and as soon as they depart, they begin to live in the aspect of eternal life of the World to Come, which is the aspect of the life of the Resurrection of the Dead. For other people do not merit that life until after [their own] purification; only then will they merit the life of the World to Come after the resurrection, each person according to his level. But the truly great tzaddikim, who completely purified their bodies in their lifetime – they merit immediately upon their passing to the eternal life of the World to Come, which is the aspect of being absorbed into the Infinite Light in the aspect of eternal life. And even there, they continue to serve Hashem, as our Sages, z”l, said. **And all their service there is to draw the Infinite Light into the heart of every Jew, in the aspect of rectifying the contractions mentioned above**, so that His kingship will be revealed to all eyes – which is the ultimate purpose of all Creation, as mentioned above. **And this is the aspect of the tremendous greatness of _hishtat’chut_ (prostrating oneself) on the graves of tzaddikim, especially on the eve of Rosh Hashana. For then one needs to draw forth the aspect of the creation of the world (which occurred on Rosh Hashana), to draw forth the aspect of rectifying creation anew, to repair the sin of Adam HaRishon which occurred then. And all of this is rectified by the aspect of rectifying the contractions, as mentioned above, which is accomplished by the great tzaddikim who have passed away**, as mentioned above.”**

Shmuel listened intently. “Very true, Yossi – that really is the matter of the _vessels_ from _Arvit 4_, even though it’s from two different Torahs in _Likutei Moharan_... And here it’s explicitly written that this is the aspect of how great the _Tziun_ (gravesite) is! In other words, Rabbeinu makes the vessels for us **by means of our hishtat’chut (prayers and connection) at the gravesite**... But one second, I didn’t know you were such a scholar, Yossi...”

Yossi looked at the two astonished young men and chuckled softly. “While you guys were busy learning how to be even more boring, I managed to finish _Likutei Halachot_ eight times.”

Shlomo raised his eyebrows in amazement.

“What are you so surprised about? Just stay consistent – two pages a day – and you can get there too. And here, further on, he talks about it even more strongly.”

**(Likutei Halachot, Hilchot Mincha 7, Paragraph 80)**

**“And this is what was explained above regarding the virtue of walking to the graves of the great tzaddikim,** for the tzaddik stood in battle during his life and shattered all the evil inclinations, etc., until through his deeds he merited to reveal His kingship, may He be blessed, in the world – thereby elevating also the Upper Realm, as mentioned above. And then on the day of his passing he merits to ascend higher and higher, to the place he ascends to. And there, there are no deeds or actions through which to reveal His kingship (for that he now merely delights in Hashem, etc. – fortunate is he). For then, with that awakening, the tzaddik does what he does and elevates him...** even though that person did not refine his character traits – on the contrary, he severely corrupted them. **For the tzaddik has the power to fix everything, by virtue of his tremendous toil and self-sacrifice in this world through sufferings and difficult mortifications for the sake of Hashem and for the sake of Israel, until he made _teshuvah_ on behalf of all of Israel for all generations.** And just as we find that Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai said, ‘I can exempt the whole world... and if Yotam ben Uzziah were with me, I could exempt it from the beginning until the end,’ etc. – **only, the main thing is that the person in this world should have at least some awakening from below, as mentioned.
